Exercise Malabar–Indo-Pacific 26 launched at 23:00 UTC across two carrier strike groups. Australian, Indian, Japanese and US assets are deployed. HMAS Hobart and HMAS Sydney are both participating — the first time two Australian air-warfare destroyers have joined a Quad-led exercise simultaneously.

The timing is conspicuous. Philippine Foreign Secretary Maria Theresa Lazaro and Chinese Vice Foreign Minister Sun Weidong began bilateral consultations in Beijing the same morning, the first such meeting since the December grounding incident at Second Thomas Shoal.

02Capital implication

Insurance markets responded within hours. Lloyd's war-risk underwriters lifted Philippine port surcharges by 12 basis points before noon Singapore time. Container freight on the trans-Pacific eastbound is repricing modestly, and roughly a third of global container traffic transits these waters.

Trans-Pacific freight reaction
+0.4% on the Drewry WCI

Marginal but coherent. Asia-to-USWC spot rates moved within five hours. Our forecast engine signals a 1.5–2.5% repricing if the exercise extends beyond its scheduled end.

For corporates with operations in the Indo-Pacific, the signalling matters more than the manoeuvres. The exercise is too brief to change operational facts; it is long enough to shape the Hanoi negotiating posture.

03The Hanoi context

The bilateral consultations in Beijing mark a thaw that few in the diplomatic community had priced in before March. Vice Foreign Minister Sun's appointment to the meeting — rather than a more junior counterpart — is itself a signal. Lazaro's brief, according to two officials briefed on the agenda, is to secure clarity on Beijing's position regarding resupply operations to the BRP Sierra Madre at Second Thomas Shoal.

The Method · How Meridian works

Two engines. One judgment.

Meridian pairs two proprietary engines. The forecast engine projects the numbers — what the data is already saying. The scenario engine rehearses the narrative — what people might do. Where they agree, conviction is high. Where they diverge, the brief flags it.

Quantitative and qualitative.

Most intelligence services run on one model. Meridian runs both, paired so that what people might do is always priced against what the data is already saying.

Engine 01 · Quantitative

Forecast Engine

A pretrained foundation model for numeric time-series. Feed it a sequence of numbers — prices, yields, freight rates, insurance surcharges, story-mention velocity — and it forecasts the next N points with calibrated confidence bands.

Zero-shot: no retraining required for new data. 200M-parameter decoder-only architecture with a 16,384-point context window and a continuous quantile head for probabilistic output up to 1,000 horizon steps. Pre-trained on 100 billion real-world data points. Top-3 zero-shot on the Monash and GIFT-Eval benchmarks. Covariate support means we can condition a forecast on related variables — FX, oil, volatility — so a forecast on Lloyd's war-risk surcharge isn't produced in isolation. Deployed via the Meridian cloud stack.

  • MethodDecoder transformer · zero-shot
  • Context16,384 data points
  • HorizonUp to 1,000 steps ahead
  • OutputQuantile bands · covariates
  • RefreshEvery 15 minutes
  • CadenceReal-time · per-story
Engine 02 · Qualitative

Scenario Engine

A multi-agent simulation engine. Spawns thousands of autonomous AI agents with distinct personalities and memories, drops them into a simulated digital world, and watches the emergent narrative over 30 days of compressed simulated time.

We feed it the same seed material the brief is built on — wire copy, official statements, policy drafts. A knowledge graph extracts entities and relationships; agent personas are generated with personality, memory and behavioural rules; they interact across simulated social and political environments; the emergent patterns are synthesised into weighted outcome paths. We treat the output as a stress-test for blind spots, not a prediction — and we say so on every brief. The engine can scale to one million agents; we typically run between 8,000 and 12,000 per brief.

  • MethodAgent-based simulation
  • KnowledgeEntity graph · memory-aware
  • Scale8,000 → 1,000,000 agents
  • Horizon30-day compressed time
  • OutputWeighted scenario paths
  • CadencePer Tier I brief · on demand

Where each engine earns its keep — and where it doesn't.

We don't oversell either tool. Here's the honest picture of when each contributes signal and when each adds noise.

Forecast Engine · Strengths

Solid on dense numeric series.

Use it for: commodity prices, FX, yields, freight indices, insurance surcharges, any well-behaved daily or hourly series with months of history. Top-3 zero-shot on the Monash benchmark, beats ARIMA, ETS, DeepAR.

Don't use it for: sparse series (X-engagement on a small story), regime breaks (the model can't know about a war that started yesterday), or anything where the historical pattern has structurally changed in the past 30 days.

Scenario Engine · Caveats

A scenario rehearsal — not a crystal ball.

Useful for: identifying blind spots, surfacing low-probability tail scenarios that human analysts dismiss, stress-testing how a story might evolve over 30–60 days. Our own research and the academic literature on agent-based simulation agree: it works best as a "rehearse the future" tool.

Known limits: LLM agents polarise faster than real humans; they inherit RLHF "consensus" bias; they don't model genuine novelty well. We treat the output as a structured prompt for human judgment, not as a forecast. Probabilities are simulation frequency, not market-implied probability.

Where the pairing earns its place.

Three concrete patterns where the forecast engine and the scenario engine together produce signals neither could deliver alone.

Application 01

Insurance and freight repricing

A geopolitical event triggers a Lloyd's surcharge move. The forecast engine projects the path of the surcharge over 7 days, using FX, oil and freight as covariates. The scenario engine simulates 8,000 agents reacting to the event over 30 days. Where they agree, conviction is high. Where they diverge, the brief flags it for the reader.

Application 02

Central-bank communication shifts

Statement language change detected. The forecast engine projects AUD/USD, ASX 200 and short-end yields with 80% bands. The scenario engine runs the new wording through agents representing market makers, hedge fund desks and the financial press to test how the narrative will travel. Output: implied policy path with a sanity check on positioning risk.

Application 03

Story acceleration and regime breaks

Mention velocity on an entity exits the forecast engine's 90% confidence band. The orchestrator promotes the story to Tier I, triggers a scenario run on the underlying event to produce a 30-day outcome tree, and routes a watch-list alert to subscribers tracking that entity. The signal is the regime break itself.

Featured dossier · Issue No. 04
Published 14 April 2026 · 14-minute read

The second decoupling.

What the first round of strategic trade restrictions taught boardrooms about the next one — and what it means for principals with exposure on both sides.

When the first export-control package dropped in late 2022, almost every boardroom we briefed treated it as an event. Eighteen months later, the same rooms are asking a different question. Not what happened, but what the shape of the decade now is. The answer coming back from the data is that the first decoupling was the rehearsal. The second — already underway in biotech, rare earths, sub-seven-nanometre fabrication, payments infrastructure and cloud AI — will be broader, slower, and far more consequential for corporates than the first.

By The Council Editorial Board Reviewed by P. Shanmugan 14 min · 5,820 words 4 data exhibits
Key finding
3 of 7sectors
In the second decoupling are not on any public export-control list yet. Biotech, payments rails and AI compute lead the quiet expansion.
